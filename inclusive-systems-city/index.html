Collaborative City Strategy Game

Inclusive Systems City

A cooperative city-management simulation where every round tests inclusion, resilience, and public trust.

Designed by Zacharoula Zempili
PhD Researcher, KTH Royal Institute of Technology

Format: 2-5 players per room

Protocol: Polling architecture

Standard: Inclusive design aligned, WCAG 2.1 AA oriented interface

Mission Briefing

Steer the city through cascading civic technology crises.

Each room progresses through twenty scenarios. Participants act in defined institutional roles, cast secret pre-votes, review aggregate divergence, deliberate under time pressure, and commit to a final decision that shifts citywide system indicators.

What This Game Trains

Inclusive Systems City is a cooperative strategy game about institutional decision-making under inclusive design pressure.

  • Inclusive governance trade-offs
  • Consensus under uncertainty
  • Risk balancing across legal, technical, and social dimensions
  • Confidence-aware decision logging for research and reflection

Standards Alignment

  • WCAG 2.1 AA-aware interaction design
  • Accessible keyboard and focus behavior
  • High contrast mode and semantic structure
  • Institutional framing suitable for research and teaching contexts

Learning Outcomes

  • Interpret inclusive design crises as cross-functional governance problems rather than isolated defects.
  • Compare private judgment, group deliberation, and final consensus under time pressure.
  • Recognize how inclusion, legal exposure, technical debt, public trust, and budget interact over time.
  • Use confidence scoring as a reflection tool for decision quality and uncertainty.

Research Context

This simulation supports structured observation of collaborative decision-making in inclusive design governance contexts.

  • Room-based participation with unique roles
  • Private pre-votes and final decisions
  • Vote shift tracking and divergence index calculation
  • Consensus timing and indicator deltas
  • Confidence score export for post-session analysis

How to Play

This guide walks through the full experience in the order players encounter it, so first-time participants know what each screen means and why each step exists.

Step 1

Enter a Room

What you will see: the options to start a new game or join a running game, plus role selection.

What you do: choose a display name, pick a unique role, and either create a room or join with a Room ID.

Why this step exists: every player enters the simulation from a different institutional perspective, so the room needs a clear role structure before discussion begins.

Note: each role can be assigned to only one player in the room. If you choose a role that is already taken, the system will ask you to pick a different one before you can continue.

Step 2

Wait in the Lobby

What you will see: the Lobby panel, the current team, room readiness, and the City Systems baseline scores.

What you do: confirm who is in the room, check your role, and wait for the host to begin the session.

Why this step exists: the lobby is the staging area that makes sure the team is complete and everyone starts from the same baseline conditions.

Note: this is the right moment to check that your role is correct. If you joined with the wrong role or wrong room, it is better to leave and re-enter before the session starts.

Step 3

Crisis Reveal

What you will see: a new crisis scenario, its category, and a set of response options.

What you do: read the scenario carefully and compare the available response options before committing to a position.

Why this step exists: it introduces the institutional problem the team must govern together under pressure.

Note: do not rush past this screen. The response options may sound similar, but they often produce very different downstream effects on the City Systems indicators.

Step 4

Secret Individual Pre-Vote

What you will see: the same response options in a private voting form, plus a confidence rating from 1 to 5.

What you do: choose the option you personally support before group discussion begins and record how confident you feel.

Why this step exists: it captures each player’s initial judgment before social influence changes the discussion.

Note: your pre-vote is not recorded until you submit both an option and a confidence rating. If one of these is missing, the system will not accept the entry.

Step 5

Aggregated Reveal

What you will see: the team’s initial voting pattern, without exposing who chose what, plus divergence metrics.

What you do: review where the group is aligned and where opinions differ.

Why this step exists: it helps the team understand whether consensus is close or whether real disagreement still needs to be worked through.

Note: the aggregated reveal does not identify individual players. It is meant to guide discussion, not expose who voted for which option.

Step 6

Timed Deliberation

What you will see: a discussion area where the team can exchange short notes, with the timer shown only if enabled.

What you do: explain your reasoning, challenge assumptions, and negotiate a shared response.

Why this step exists: the simulation is about collaborative governance, so the discussion phase is where trade-offs become visible and negotiable.

Note: in timed mode, phases advance automatically when the countdown ends. In untimed mode, the phase waits until every member of the room has chosen to continue, so the whole group still moves together.

Step 7

Final Decision

What you will see: the final voting form with the same options and a second confidence rating.

What you do: submit your final choice after discussion and indicate how confident you are now.

Why this step exists: it shows whether players changed their minds, how consensus formed, and how discussion affected confidence.

Note: your final decision is also only saved after both fields are completed. If you change your mind during discussion, make sure the currently selected option is still the one you want before submitting.

Step 8

Explanation, Impact, and System Update

What you will see: the expert explanation, the impact summary, and the updated City Systems indicators.

What you do: review what the chosen response means and observe how the city indicators shift.

Why this step exists: this is where the game connects decisions to consequences across inclusion, legal risk, trust, stability, and budget.

Note: some indicators are healthier when they go up, while others are healthier when they go down. Read the indicator guidance carefully before assuming every larger number is better.

Step 9

Continue Across Twenty Crises

What you will see: the round counter and the City Systems evolving over time.

What you do: repeat the cycle for each new crisis while watching how earlier decisions shape later conditions.

Why this step exists: the simulation is cumulative, so patterns emerge across the full session rather than from one crisis alone.

Note: before the round begins, agree as a group whether you want timed play or untimed play. Timed play creates automatic transitions, while untimed play requires all members to signal readiness before the next step opens.
